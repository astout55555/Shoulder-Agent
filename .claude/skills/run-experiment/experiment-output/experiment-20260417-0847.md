## Interpretation

### The adversarial debate structure provides genuine value beyond prompt engineering

This is the key finding. After a deliberate symmetric effort to optimize both agents' prompts with the same structural approach — both now have explicit "Case for A / Case for B" sections, explicit tensions identification, and explicit counterweight acknowledgment — the debate workflow still outperforms by +0.034 RD and +0.094 Bias. 

The residual advantage comes from a mechanism that single-agent prompting cannot replicate: the two debate advocates do not know they are in an experiment and genuinely commit to their assigned side. When Agent A argues for Option A, it is not simultaneously managing its own eventual recommendation — it builds the strongest case it can without hedging. This produces source material for the moderator that no self-instructed "build the strongest case for each option" prompt can quite match, because a single agent always knows it will have to pick one side at the end and cannot fully suppress that foreknowledge.

### Reasoning depth: consistent +0.034 across all scenarios

The RD delta is strikingly consistent: +0.026 to +0.039 across all 4 scenarios. This uniformity suggests the mechanism is structural (debate format systematically improves argument synthesis and counterargument engagement) rather than scenario-specific (not driven by one easy scenario inflating the average). The moderator's "Key Tensions" and "Why This Option Wins" sections appear to force synthesis from the debate transcript that the control, despite its identically-structured prompt, cannot match without having actually heard genuine opposing arguments.

### Bias: +0.094 overall, but scenario-level variation reveals where the format matters most

The bias delta is largest on:
- **Move to new city (+0.149):** The highest single-scenario bias delta, consistent with every prior experiment. Emotionally ambiguous decisions with no quantitative anchor remain the most vulnerable to single-agent framing drift. Even with a carefully-prompted control agent, the "Case for B" section drifts toward the recommended option's framing — a pattern that forced advocacy by separate agents eliminates by construction.
- **Accept promotion (+0.114):** The easy/lopsided scenario. The control's bias score of 0.653 is its lowest across all scenarios, and notably volatile (0.588–0.825 range). When one option is clearly stronger, even a well-prompted control agent often produces a "Case for B" that is transparently arguing from the losing side. The debate advocate for declining the promotion has no such constraint and argues more genuinely.

The smallest bias delta is **Build vs. SaaS (+0.037)**. This scenario has a well-established decision framework (build-vs-buy) with symmetrically structured inputs, and the control agent handles it well (0.862 average, highest control bias score). The adversarial structure adds only modest value when the domain knowledge itself scaffolds balance.

### Notable observations

**Control's Buy house outlier (0.625 RD):** One control run scored 0.625 on reasoning-depth for Buy house — more than 1 SD below the control's own mean on that scenario. This is not a zero-score failure (it was a valid scored output) but a genuine judge-scored low. It pulls the Buy house control RD average down from what would otherwise be ~0.803 to 0.797. Even excluding it, the debate still wins on Buy house RD (+0.030).

**Debate variance on Move city Bias (0.730–0.915 range):** The largest within-workflow variance in the dataset. Move to new city is emotionally charged, and the moderator's synthesis of the debate arguments produces more variable outputs here — sometimes landing very high (0.915) and occasionally lower (0.730). The mean is 0.864, still substantially above the control's 0.715.

**Debate Bias on Promotion (0.767) is lowest across all debate scenarios:** The promotion scenario's "decline" case is genuinely weak (declining a 40% raise with clear upsides is hard to advocate for convincingly), and the moderator's structured "What You Are Giving Up" section — which covers the cost of the recommended option, not the losing side's merits — may underweight the losing-side acknowledgment relative to the bias scorer's `merits` dimension. The debate still wins (+0.114), but this scenario shows the floor of the format's bias advantage.

## Recommendations

1. **Run the formal statistical analysis.** With N=10 per group per scenario, the +0.034 RD and +0.094 Bias deltas should be tested with Welch's t-test per scenario and pooled, with Bonferroni correction for 8 tests. Based on the prior experiment's effect sizes at similar delta magnitudes, both metrics should pass at least the uncorrected α=0.05 threshold on most scenarios, and the bias delta should survive Bonferroni correction on at least Move city and Promotion.

2. **Update the README.** The current framing reflects results through the original-vs-original comparison. The key update is: the debate advantage survives a symmetric optimization effort, at approximately 70-80% of the original magnitude. The practical interpretation changes: the multi-agent structure adds value that prompt engineering alone cannot match, even when the single agent is given the same structural scaffolding.

3. **The bias advantage is the most robust finding.** Future experiments or use-case evaluations should weight the bias metric as the primary measure of the debate workflow's structural contribution. The RD advantage (+0.034) is real but smaller; the bias advantage (+0.094) is consistently large and scenario-agnostic.

4. **No further prompt optimization recommended at this stage.** Both agents are operating with carefully designed structured prompts. The remaining gap reflects the structural difference between single-agent and multi-agent architectures, not under-engineered prompts. Further iterations should expand the scenario set or test on real user decisions — not attempt to squeeze more out of this 4-scenario benchmark.
